My Phone Approves Code Now

By Wesley Melo · April 24, 2026 · 3 min read

OpenAI shipped mobile supervision for Codex inside the ChatGPT app on May 14. I tried it from a bus stop on a Tuesday, which is the kind of sentence I would have rolled my eyes at three years ago.

The flow is exactly as advertised. The agent does its thing in the cloud. Your phone vibrates. You tap. You read a diff that you do not understand because diffs on a four-inch screen are a war crime. You tap again. You have approved a database migration with your thumb.

I. The product is real

This is going to be huge for a specific kind of person and useless for everyone else. The person it’s built for is the founder-engineer running three repos who needs to keep things moving while standing in a coffee queue. Mobile supervision lets them be the bottleneck without being at the desk.

The person it’s not built for is anyone who has ever read a diff carefully. The screen is the screen. The decisions you make on it will be worse than the decisions you make at the desk. That’s fine for a green-light on a doc fix and not fine for anything that touches money or users.

You have approved a database migration with your thumb. Welcome to your new career.

II. What I keep thinking about

The real shift is not the phone — it’s the implicit contract. The agent is now allowed to wait for you. It will pause a long-running job, batch up its questions, and ping you when it needs adult supervision. Approvals are async. The work runs while you sleep.

This is the part of agentic coding that people keep underselling. We’re not just removing typing; we’re removing presence. The job becomes: be reachable, be decisive, be right. Which is, depressingly, also the job of a manager.

If you have ever wondered what your engineering managers actually do, the answer is starting to leak into your text messages.
