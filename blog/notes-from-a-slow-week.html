Notes from a Slow Week

On friction, boredom, and the quiet engineering of patience. A short field report from a week of deliberately doing less.

WM
Wesley Melo
February 5, 2026 · 3 min read

Last week I did an experiment that, in retrospect, sounds like the opening line of a TED talk I would never give: I closed every productivity tool on my laptop and tried to work like it was 2014. No agents. No autocomplete on the autocomplete. No little side panel suggesting I “summarize” the email I just wrote. Just me, a text editor, and the gathering suspicion that I have been a productivity tool for the productivity tools.

It went both better and worse than I expected, which is the only honest review of any experiment.

The spreadsheet felt my absence (it did not)

The numbers on the AI-at-work side of things have started to wobble in a way that I find clarifying. There’s a story going around right now about Amazon employees gaming their internal “AI usage” metrics, basically inventing little tasks to feed the assistant so the dashboard goes green. There’s a separate, broader thesis (call it AI psychosis at the org level) that says many companies have collectively convinced themselves the tools are working much better than they actually are, because the alternative is to admit they spent the year on a vibe.

I am not anti-tool. (I write this on a laptop that costs more than my first car, with a model running in the background that I will absolutely use to spell-check this piece. Please do not @ me.) But the gap between “the tool is great” and “I am great using the tool” has gotten very wide, and a slow week was the only way I could see the edge of it.

Productivity theater has an intermission

The first two days were unhinged. I kept reaching for the shortcut, the autocomplete, the “rewrite this in a friendlier tone” button. I have type-six-hyphens-per-sentence Disease and my brain wanted help editing it down. I had to physically sit on my hands at one point. (Reader, this is humiliating to admit. Reader, I’m not lying.)

By day three the thing I’d been hiding from showed up. The thing was: a lot of my “output” over the last year had been the residue of having a co-pilot, not the residue of having thought about anything. The Slack messages that pinged out polished. The doc that arrived 40% drafted by Tuesday morning. The pull request comment that was diplomatic in a way I’m not, in person, ever. None of it was bad. Almost none of it was mine.

Then by day five something quieter happened. I started writing slower, but the sentences started landing harder. The doc had fewer headers but the headers meant something. I sent a message to a teammate that was twelve words long and got back “thank you, that’s exactly what I needed,” which is the rarest reply in software.

The output got worse on paper and better in the room. That is a metric we do not have a dashboard for.

Throwing some ice at the fever

A few numbers, since we’re here:

The average knowledge worker now touches around seven distinct AI-assisted tools in a normal week. A separate survey says about one in three of those workers admits to fabricating activity to satisfy a tool’s tracking. Meanwhile, the average length of a focused work block has fallen below twenty minutes for the first time since the metric was invented. Reader, twenty minutes is not a work block. Twenty minutes is a microwave timer.

“If you’re not 10x-ing with agents, you’re obsolete by Q3.”

A Tech Guru on X

Sure. Q3 will be here in a minute. I’ll be the obsolete person writing a sentence on a Tuesday and meaning it.

So, is slow a flex?

I’m not going to tell you to delete your tools. (I’m not going to tell you to keep them either; that’s a sponsorship I have not been offered.) What I will say is that a slow week worked on me like a tuning fork. It reminded me what the “before” state of a sentence feels like, before three rounds of suggestion polish it into being maximally readable and minimally surprising.

The next economy is going to reward the people who can still notice the difference. Not because slowness is virtuous (it is not, it is mostly inconvenient) but because the ability to feel the difference between “the tool wrote this” and “I meant this” is going to be the new literacy. You can not learn it without the receipts that a slow week gives you.

Some things even AI can not help with. The list includes: realizing your last six Slack messages did not sound like you, deciding the meeting actually should be a walk, telling your teammate the thing you’ve been afraid to say. We are going to need more practice at all of those, soon, very fast.

Now if you’ll excuse me, I have to go close the seventeen tabs I opened during this draft. By hand. Like an animal.
