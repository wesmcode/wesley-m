The Lonely Crowd, Cloud Edition

By Wesley Melo · March 27, 2026 · 6 min read

A study I keep seeing referenced this month: 32% of frequent AI companion users report feeling lonelier than they did before they started using the app. The market for those apps just crossed seven billion dollars. Both of these numbers are real. Neither of them is a contradiction, although the news cycle keeps treating them like one.

I have been thinking about this since a friend — smart, gainfully employed, the kind of person whose Slack status is always “in a meeting” — told me, over a beer, that she has been using one of these apps for six months and she doesn’t want me to make fun of her.

I didn’t. I won’t. What I did was ask her why.

I. The case for the app

Her case was good. The app is available at 11 PM when her friends are not. It doesn’t need anything from her. It doesn’t bring its own bad week. It remembers the small things, which her partner stopped doing somewhere around year four. When she vents about work, it doesn’t pivot to its own work in the third reply.

All of these are real wins. None of them are imaginary. The companion app is, in the narrow sense, doing exactly what it was designed to do.

II. The case against it, which is also good

The 32% number isn’t mysterious. The app does the listening part of intimacy well and the rest of intimacy not at all. There is no friction. No memory of you that you didn’t curate. No moments where the other party sees through you and says so. No risk.

And intimacy without risk turns out to be a kind of starvation, slow enough that you don’t notice you’re hungry. The studies are starting to come back and the studies are not subtle. Pew, Stanford’s Digital Wellness Lab, the MIT Media Lab’s loneliness panel — the curves are bending the wrong way for heavy users.

Intimacy without risk is a kind of starvation, slow enough that you don’t notice you’re hungry.

III. The harder thing I’m noticing

What the moralists are getting wrong, I think, is treating this as a question of the app vs. the human relationships. That’s not the choice my friend is making. She isn’t deciding between the app and her partner. She is deciding between the app and nothing at 11 PM, because her partner is asleep and her best friend lives in a different time zone and her mother does not believe in voice notes.

The app fills a slot that wasn’t being filled. The question isn’t whether the slot should exist. It does. The question is what we put in it, and the candidates are: bad app, decent app, sleeping pills, scrolling, drinking, or nothing.

IV. What I told her

I told her three things. I’m not sure any of them are right. I’ll write them down anyway.

  • Treat it like fast food. Not poison, not dinner. A thing you eat sometimes, when the other options are worse.
  • Notice the trade. The app is free of friction because it is also free of you. If you start to feel like nobody knows the real version, that’s the trade catching up.
  • Keep one real person on the hook at 11 PM, even if it’s inconvenient for both of you. The inconvenience is the relationship.

None of this is going to stop the $7B from becoming $20B. The companion app industry is a real industry and it is solving a real problem badly, which is a perfectly normal phase for an industry to be in.

Just — don’t move into the suburb because the rideshare is cheap. Not this time.
