You Are Not Paying for AI. You Are Paying for the Subsidy.

A Medium post made the rounds this week arguing the obvious. Why it's worth saying anyway.

WM
Wesley Melo
April 18, 2026 · 6 min read

Jose Ruacho's post, You're Not Paying for AI. You're Paying for a Subsidy, bounced around Medium and into the Hacker News front page last week, and I think it deserves the second read it isn't getting.

The point is plain. Your twenty-dollar subscription is not the cost of the service. It is a friendly downpayment on the price the service will eventually have to charge you. The gap between those two numbers is being filled by venture capital that is, by some estimates, the largest single capital flow into a software category in living memory.

I. We have been here before

I have seen this movie. Anyone who lived through the 2010s has. Uber lost money on every ride and made it back on the IPO. WeWork lost money on every desk and made it back on the storytelling. The pattern is so old now it has its own genre conventions:

  • Stage one: free or cheap. The product feels magical. Users get hooked. Reviewers swoon.
  • Stage two: a price hike that is not framed as a price hike. New tiers, "Pro" features, quietly throttled free plans.
  • Stage three: enterprise contracts and ads. The product is now mediocre and twice the price, and you are still on it because the switching cost is brutal.
  • Stage four: someone writes a long retrospective.

We are currently late in stage one and entering stage two. The signs are everywhere if you squint: usage caps that didn't exist three months ago. Credit pools. Mysterious model deprecations that nudge you toward the more expensive tier.

The product is a loss leader. You are the marketing. Both of those statements are normal. Neither of them is comforting.

II. Why I don't mind, exactly

I don't hate the subsidy. I lived through Uber-cheap and I knew it was Uber-cheap, and I took the rides anyway. The subsidy is not a moral failing on the user's part. It's a temporary mispricing, and you are allowed to enjoy temporary mispricings.

What you are not allowed to do is forget about it. The thing that makes the Uber-era painful in retrospect is not that we used cheap rides. It is that we built whole habits on top of them (commutes, dating patterns, late-night logistics, restaurant economics) and then watched the price snap back and the habits not budge.

III. The personal hedge

The hedge I'm running, quietly: assume the pricing doubles in eighteen months. Assume the most useful model gets locked behind a tier that is three times what I pay today. Then ask, of every workflow I'm building on top of it: does this still make sense at the real price?

Most of mine do. A few don't. The ones that don't are the ones that were a parlor trick anyway: nice demos that never became real value. The subsidy was hiding them. When it lifts, they go.

IV. The part that should worry the platforms

Here's the only twist the playbook hasn't accounted for: open-weights are real. They are getting good. A version of the model that is "80% as good for 5% of the cost" will exist for every model that anyone is currently paying for, and it will exist within twelve months of the pricing snap.

Which means the snap may not be a snap. It may be a cliff the providers themselves can't walk off of. That is genuinely new. I have no idea how it ends.

But the subscription will not stay this cheap. The only people who think it will are the people who did not live through Uber, and to them I say: enjoy the rides. Just don't move to the suburbs because of one.
