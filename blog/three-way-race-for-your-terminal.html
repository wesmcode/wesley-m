The Three-Way Race for Your Terminal

Claude Code, Codex CLI, and Grok Build are all asking to live in your shell. Same tickets, same constraints, same long-suffering Postgres test database.

For the last thirty days I have been rotating between Claude Code, Codex CLI, and xAI's newly-announced Grok Build on the same codebase. Same tickets. Same constraints. Same long-suffering Postgres test database. This is the field report nobody asked me for, and which I am writing anyway.

A short disclaimer up top: I am not a benchmark. I am one developer doing real work, and my impressions are shaped by my taste and my history with each company. Take what you like.

I. Claude Code

The one I keep coming back to. The plan-first habit suits the way my brain works. I want a model that pauses before it sprints. Claude Code asks more questions than the others, sometimes annoyingly so, and the questions are usually the right ones.

Where it shines: long refactors across many files, anything with a real test suite, anything where the cost of a wrong edit is high.

Where it stumbles: short, vibe-y tasks. I sometimes want a tool that will just do the thing. Claude Code wants to discuss it first. That can feel like booking a meeting to ask a question.

II. Codex CLI

OpenAI's entry feels like the second draft of a product they already shipped, in a good way. It is fast. It is opinionated. It is willing to run a command without explaining why, which I respect.

The May 14 mobile supervision update changed the calculus a bit. If you want the agent to keep moving while you are away from the desk, Codex now has the best story for that. I covered the experience separately, but the short version: handy for the right person, terrifying for the wrong one.

Where it shines: speed runs, anything CI-adjacent, anything where the loop is "try, fail, try again."

Where it stumbles: judgment calls. When the task is ambiguous, Codex picks an interpretation, commits to it, and you find out later. Sometimes you wanted that. Sometimes you did not.

III. Grok Build

The new one. Earnest, scrappy, slightly raw. The DevOps.com piece introducing it called it a "coding agent for engineers who think marketing is a lie," which is exactly the tone the product carries.

Advertisement

It is fast in a way that suggests less safety net than the other two. It will, on occasion, do something that makes me reach for the undo with feeling. I have also seen it solve problems the other two danced around, in part because it does not pause to think about whether it should.

Where it shines: greenfield work, experimentation, anything where the cost of being wrong is small.

Where it stumbles: production. I would not give this thing prod credentials in its current form, but I would say the same of a junior engineer on day one. It will mature.

Three tools in your terminal. Three different theories of what an engineer is for. You pick the one whose theory you can live with.

IV. The pattern under the differences

The interesting thing is not which tool wins. The interesting thing is that each company has a slightly different theory of what the engineer is for, and the tool reflects it.

  • Anthropic's theory: the engineer is a reviewer and a chooser. The model is a tireless drafting partner that wants your sign-off.
  • OpenAI's theory: the engineer is an operator. The model is a fleet of agents you point at problems and check on later.
  • xAI's theory: the engineer is a hacker. The model is a wrench you swing without permission.

None of these is wrong. They are different stances on the same future, and the team you work on will lean naturally toward one of them whether or not you discuss it out loud.

V. What I will keep on my machine

Claude Code for the work I care about. Codex CLI for the work I want done while I am getting coffee. Grok Build for the experiments I would be embarrassed for anyone to see.

Three tools is one too many. I will drop one of them by August. Do not ask me which.
