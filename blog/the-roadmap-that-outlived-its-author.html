The Roadmap That Outlived Its Author

By Wesley Melo · February 12, 2026 · 5 min read

The 2026 Agentic Coding Trends Report did its annual rounds in February, and the trade press did its annual thing of asking what does the PM role look like now? as if the answer is going to be meaningfully different from last year. It mostly isn’t. But one thing has shifted, and I think it’s the thing worth writing about.

The shift is who reads your roadmap. For the first time, it isn’t just other humans.

I. The new audience

In every team I’m advising right now, there is an agentic coding system — Claude Code, Codex, something internal — that is, somewhere in its context, looking at the team’s planning docs. Not always officially. Sometimes a junior engineer pasted last week’s PRD into a prompt and the agent has been quietly carrying it ever since.

This is the first generation of roadmaps where bad writing literally costs you working code. Ambiguity used to be a meeting tax. Now it’s a compile error. The roadmap is being executed, not just consumed.

II. What that changes about the job

Not much, technically. The good PMs were already writing clear, unambiguous, testable specs. They were already separating what from why from how. They were already writing as if the reader had never been in the room.

The bad PMs were writing collaboration documents — phrases stitched together to survive a meeting, never to be re-read in isolation. Those documents fall apart when an agent tries to use them. The cracks were always there. The agent just makes them obvious.

Ambiguity used to be a meeting tax. Now it’s a compile error.

III. A small ritual I’m running

Before I publish a planning doc now, I do one extra pass. I imagine handing it to a smart, willing, mildly stubborn intern who will absolutely take every sentence literally. Where would they make the wrong move?

  • What does “the user” mean here? Logged-in user? Free-tier user? The user who’s been around since the old subscription model? Pick one.
  • What does “fast” mean? p50? p95? Perceived? On what device?
  • What does “the existing design” refer to? If somebody opens the design file today, which file is it, and where is it pinned?

None of these are new questions. They’re just the questions that the room would have answered for you. The room is not always there anymore. The doc has to do the work.

IV. The part that’s actually new

The part that’s actually new, I think, is that the roadmap now has a half-life it didn’t have before. An agent reading it will keep using it long after you’ve forgotten what it said. Your March planning doc is going to show up in May, helpfully cited by a model that has no idea you’ve changed your mind.

So: date the docs. Version the docs. Mark the dead ones dead. Get into the habit of writing a single line at the top that says this supersedes [link], because the agent will not infer that from a Notion folder structure.

The PM job in 2026 is still — gloriously, mercifully — the job of stopping the team from shipping the wrong thing faster. The new wrinkle is that the team includes a few engines that don’t blink.
