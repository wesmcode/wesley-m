The Vibe Coder Confessional

By Wesley Melo · May 12, 2026 · 5 min read

Look, I need to confess something: somewhere between January and March I stopped calling myself a developer and started calling myself a vibe coder — mostly as a joke, and then a recruiter put it in a subject line and I had to lie down for a while.

The JetBrains survey going around Hacker News this week pegs the number at 90%. Ninety percent of developers now use at least one AI tool at work. The other 10% are either lying to the survey or running a mainframe in a bunker that still has carpet tiles from 1994.

The thing nobody tells you about vibe coding is that it’s not the prompting that’s hard. The prompting is easy. The hard part is the part where, every twelve minutes, you have to remember what you were originally trying to build, because the model has gently nudged you two architectural decisions to the left and you have shipped three new files for a feature you didn’t agree to.

I. A field report from my own keyboard

I tracked a week of my own work. Not because I am that organized, but because my partner asked me what I do all day and I realized I did not know.

  • Mondays: I write tickets the way a barista writes a drink order. Loud, fast, in the third person.
  • Tuesdays: the agent does 80% of the work and I do 100% of the doubt.
  • Wednesdays: I revert most of Tuesday.
  • Thursdays: I write a real function, by hand, and it feels like calligraphy.
  • Fridays: I tell a friend at a bar that I am still “in tech.”

That’s the loop. It works. It is also, if I am honest, not exactly what I thought my career was going to feel like at thirty-something.

The prompting is easy. Remembering what you were trying to build is the part that pays.

II. Why this is fine, mostly

The community pushback is real. r/programming spent April flirting with a full LLM ban (currently a trial, ask me again in two weeks). The Hacker News comment sections under every “I built X with Claude Code” post have hardened into a kind of municipal court: prove your case or pay the fine.

And yet — the work is getting done. Quietly. Inconsistently. With the occasional hallucinated function call like a raisin in an otherwise fine cookie. The agent is not a colleague. It is a very fast, very confident intern who has read every codebase ever written and remembers approximately none of them.

What the survey number really says, I think, is that we crossed a threshold. Using AI at work is no longer a signal — not bragging, not slumming. It’s just the desk. The way email was the desk in 2003. The way Slack was the desk in 2017. You can still hate it. You still have to open it.

III. The part where I’m kinder to myself

Here’s the part I keep returning to: the people I respect most in this work are the ones who can still describe, in plain English, what their software is supposed to do for the person on the other end of it. The model cannot do that for you. It can produce code that satisfies a description, but it cannot produce the description.

So — vibe code, sure. But keep the description. Keep the taste. Keep the part of you that still gets annoyed when something is almost right.

Now if you’ll excuse me, I need to revert Tuesday again.
