## 1. Three positioning archetypes

Every site I studied falls into one of three patterns. They are mutually exclusive in copy voice, even though the underlying role is similar.

### A. The Studio (Halaska Studio, character.co pattern)

- **Voice:** "We" (a small, senior team led by a named principal).
- **Avoids the word "fractional"** entirely. Says "studio," "partner," "small senior team."
- **Qualifies the client, not the seller.** Halaska: *"We work with funded teams who need design judgment in the room, not just hands on the work."* This sentence puts the seller in a position of selectivity.
- **Specific niche on the door.** Halaska: *"Product Design for AI startups."* Five words, one audience, one offer.
- **Credibility through years and brand proof.** *"Twenty years of design, six of them at Google."* *"Trusted by founders who've raised $160M+."*
- **Closing line is a manifesto, not a CTA.** Halaska: *"Let's design the right thing, the right way."*
- **No pricing.** No service grid. No "process" diagram.

### B. The Operator-Writer (Aakash Gupta, Ben Erez pattern)

- **Voice:** "I."
- **Operator credibility leads, content audience follows.** Aakash: *"VP of Product at Apollo.io during the run from $750M to $2.5B."* The newsletter subscriber count comes after, not before.
- **Sells multiple SKUs**: a course, a copilot, a newsletter, advisory hours. The personal site is a portal, not a service brochure.
- **Career-stage authority.** Wesley's resume can support this archetype but only if his published writing is doing the audience-building work in parallel, which today it is not.

### C. The Pure Fractional (thefractionalproductmanager.com, rohitkatiyar.pro, smithhorn.ca, getproductpeople.com)

- **Voice:** Mixed. Solo operators say "I" (Rohit Katiyar: *"I will help build the product roadmap"*). Agencies say "we." Both are common.
- **The word "fractional" is everywhere.** In the URL, hero, headings, CTA. Search-driven positioning.
- **Service grid of six.** Strategy, discovery, GTM, design, delivery, optimization. The catalog reads as interchangeable.
- **Tiered pricing**, often by client ARR or hours/week. Sometimes opaque, sometimes published.
- **"Embedded, not advisor"** is the most-repeated phrase across the category. Smith Horn: *"We join your Slack, attend your standups, write your specs, and ship alongside you."*
- **Speed claim.** Product People: *"start delivering value within 3 to 10 days."*
- **CTA is a 30-minute call.** Always.

## 2. What halaskastudio.com does that the fractional sites don't

Worth isolating because this is the reference the user named.

1. **Refuses the category label.** Calling itself a "studio" rather than a "fractional design firm" reframes the offer from staffing solution to creative partnership. Higher perceived ceiling.
2. **Qualifies the buyer in the hero.** "Funded teams who need design judgment in the room" filters before it sells. Lower-quality leads self-disqualify.
3. **Picks one vertical and stays there.** AI startups. The whole page rewards a single search intent.
4. **Pairs scale proof with a craft sentence.** *"$160M+ raised"* is the institutional proof. *"What to build matters"* is the craft sentence. They sit next to each other and reinforce.
5. **States the founder's CV inside the studio frame.** "Twenty years of design, six of them at Google on products used by over a billion people" is biography reframed as institutional capability.
6. **No process diagram.** No five-step funnel. No "How we work." The page assumes the reader has hired a senior designer before.
7. **One closing sentiment, not a CTA stack.** *"Let's design the right thing, the right way."* The CTA reads as a shared standard, not a button.

## 5. The "I" vs "we" question for wesley-m.com

This is the live decision the copy rewrite has to make.

**Case for "I":**
- Wesley is one person. The about page already commits to first-person.
- Rohit Katiyar, Ben Erez, Aakash Gupta, Nesrine Changuel all use "I." It is the dominant voice for solo operators.
- "I" is harder to fake. It forces concrete claims (Wesley shipped 8 agents) instead of agency abstractions ("our fractional leaders ship alongside your team").

**Case for "we" / studio framing:**
- Halaska's ceiling is higher. The studio frame is what lets a one-person operation charge studio rates.
- "We" lets Wesley present curated past collaborators (ThoughtWorks, Code and Theory teams) as the studio's range without misrepresenting employment.
- AI-product work pairs naturally with "studio" framing the way design does. The buyer is hiring a small senior team, not a contractor.

**Recommendation:** Keep "I" on about.html (Wesley's personal page). Consider testing a "studio" frame on services.html or a dedicated landing page if Wesley wants to push pricing up. The two voices can coexist on the same domain if the URL paths make clear which is which. Halaska runs the studio frame at the root and shows Chris's bio inside it.

If Wesley stays single-voiced "I" across the whole site, the move to borrow from Halaska is the *qualifying sentence* in the hero. Today the hero says *"Wesley Melo, fractional PM."* That is a category claim. Halaska's hero is a buyer filter. The equivalent for Wesley would be a sentence that names which founders he wants:

> "Senior PM for founder-led teams shipping AI in production. Two or three of you a year, a few days a week each."

That sentence does three jobs: qualifies the buyer (founder-led, shipping AI), states the size of the practice (two or three), and prices the offer in time, not dollars.

## 6. Specific moves to lift into the wesley-m.com rewrite

These slot into the existing punch list in `copy-rewrite-working.md`.

1. **Hero: replace category-label hero with a qualifying sentence.** The current draft *"Wesley Melo, fractional PM"* is the rohitkatiyar move. The halaska move is one sentence that filters buyers and states the practice size.

2. **Drop the word "fractional" from the page title, keep it in the body.** Halaska doesn't say "fractional design" on the door. The word is SEO bait but it caps positioning. Use it in metadata and in the second paragraph.

3. **Pair institutional proof with a craft sentence.** Wesley's institutional proof is "8 agents shipped in 90 days, 4 enterprise pilots." The craft sentence next to it should be one line about how he decides what to build. Halaska's pattern: a scale number sitting beside a one-line standard.

4. **Inventory the day, not the methodology.** Smith Horn's *"We join your Slack, attend your standups, write your specs, and ship alongside you"* outperforms the current Process section's "Discover / Shape / Create / Scale." Concrete day-of-the-week verbs read more honest than verbed nouns.

5. **Refuse the cost-comparison framing.** The category is full of "30 to 50% of a full-time hire." This positions the seller as a substitute good. Wesley's resume justifies positioning as a *better* good for a specific situation (founder-led, AI in production). Talking about price savings drops the floor on perceived value.

6. **Cut the service grid of six.** Wesley's services.html already lists six engagement shapes. That is the category's default. Consider compressing to three on the public page (the three that actually convert: AI sprint, fractional embed, audit) and pushing the rest into a longer FAQ or a /work-with-me detail page. Halaska doesn't list services like an order menu; it lists "We design" and gives five generic deliverable categories.

7. **Replace the testimonial section with one earned line, or remove it.** Smith Horn quotes a specific person at a specific company. Wesley's services page currently uses a self-attributed quote, which the copy doc already flagged for removal. Better to have nothing than a placeholder.

8. **Closing line: standard, not button.** Halaska's *"Let's design the right thing, the right way"* is a value, not a CTA. Wesley's equivalent would be a sentence that states the standard he holds himself to. The about-cards already gesture at this ("specs in writing, weekly read-outs, handover that runs without me"). Compress one of those into a closing sentiment that lives at the bottom of the page.

## 7. Voice tells to scrub (already on Wesley's list, now confirmed by category survey)

The copy-rewrite-working doc named these as tells. The survey confirms they are also the *category's* tells, which means they have stopped reading as voice and started reading as wallpaper.

- "Drives" / "drive" as a verb
- "Aligns" / "alignment"
- "Unblocks engineering"
- "Seamlessly"
- "Strategic direction"
- "Measurable results"
- "Without the overhead of a full-time hire"
- "Senior" as an adjective on the door (use it sparingly inside the body, never in the H1)
- "Embedded, not advisor" (true; exhausted)

Replace with concrete verbs from the actual week: "I sit through your sprint planning. I write the spec. I send the Friday read-out."
